Reading Chip Huyen's "Designing Machine Learning Systems" perfectly aligned with my current project: building a CPU-only, self-hosted deployment pipeline for medical image segmentation.

The task involves the PUMA dataset. The goal is to segment tumor tissue and identify tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) in advanced melanoma. My architecture uses a frozen DINOv2 backbone with a lightweight trained head.

My hardware was strict: CPU-only, no GPU. I set myself a target of under 1 second for inference.

Applying the book's deployment concepts, I tested seven optimization techniques on a real validation set to hit this target without destroying accuracy: dynamic quantization, ONNX Runtime, lower input resolution, hybrid resolution, low-rank factorization, structured pruning, and knowledge distillation.

Only two approaches broke the 1-second barrier (see chart). The biggest surprise was knowledge distillation. Training a 16x smaller student model to mimic the DINOv2 backbone retained 76% of clinical accuracy. This easily outperformed shrinking the input resolution, which dropped accuracy to 61%.

This performance required only 8 training epochs. There is significant room to improve by further training and fine-tuning the head jointly.

The book flags distillation as a high-risk compression technique for accuracy. In this CPU-bound scenario, it proved to be the optimal choice.
